Experience Analysis

1) What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

The site begins with an intro/loading sequence. I tested it on a few different platforms; using Safari on a Mac desktop the site loaded almost immediately, and it took around the same amount of time using Chrome on a windows laptop. However, when using Safari on iPad and iOS the site loads for around a minute and a half before repeatedly crashing once the user begins to scroll. The loading screen (when you actually have time to see it) is pretty engaging, showing the building being sketched.

2) Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

The first segment of the experience has the user moving through drone footage of the Walt Disney Concert Hall. It then shows the user a series of videos detailing the initial design of the concert hall, alongside a button that when clicked brings up a 3D model of the hall's interior that can be viewed at from multiple angles and clicked on in certain locations to reveal further information and images. The next segment shows a chapter title, then a short section that most closely resembles a traditional news/education article. The user is then shown another series of videos, alongside a button that allows them to explore a 3d model of the hall's exterior that functions the same as the previous one. The user then scrolls through more article-like text and images, broken up by dynamic headers that change shape as the user scrolls through them. These article sections aren't completely flat, there are elements that grow and shrink as the user scrolls, and parts of the site that stay fixed on one side as the other half of the page scrolls. There is then a third video section followed by a model of the concert hall's organ that functions the same way as the previous two. This is then followed by the final article section of the site, and then a brief scroll through drone footage flying away from the building to bookmark the experience. There is then an in-depth index of archived media from the designing of the building. This section can be expanded for more information. The site ends with an acknowledgements and credits section. Through the whole experience, the sound toggle and menu buttons stay pinned to the top right of the site. The menu button brings up a link to each chapter when pressed.

3) What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

Overall I think the sections of the site I spent the most time on were the video portions, simply because they take their time to explain the details of the design process. They weren't that interactive though, apart from being able to easily skip to the next/previous video by clicking on the left/right side of the screen. The parts I spent the most time on that I would actually consider interactive were the 3D models - I was interested in the story so I spent time going through each module to learn more.

4) What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

The site uses scrolling as its primary form of interaction. There are also moments in which the user is encouraged to click for more information or drag a 3D model around, but from the beginning to the end the user scrolls through the page in order to experience the story.

5) What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

I think the main goal of the experience is to educate people about the creation of the Walt Disney Concert Hall in a manner that is engaging, unique, and fun.

6) How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

The experience is pretty immediately interactive and playful, showing the way it intends to educate the user. Even for a user not interested in the content of the site, it's hard not to have a good time simply scrolling through the footage, videos, and 3d models.

7) What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

I think the experience is an engaging, fairly short experience best suited for those interested in architectural design and how it relates to music. Something unique about this site is that it could take a good amount more time to experience depending on the user's investment in the content. For an uninterested user there are plenty of opportunities to simply skip through information. On the flip side, for someone particularly invested there are equally as many opportunities to learn much more than a typical site might show. That being said, I can't imagine this being a site people would revisit over and over. It tells an interesting story, but not one infinitely repeatable.

8) How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

The site is happy to let the user skip through at their leisure - it would rather the user stay to watch the videos, as there are distinct progress bars showing how far through the content the user is alongside warnings of "leaving the interactive story" that appear if the user scrolls past the video content without finishing it. Ultimately though the experience doesn't really change based on if you want to speed through or take it slow. At no point does it lock the user anywhere.

9) What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

The experience explicitly references 67 archived pieces in the index at the bottom of the page. These pieces include photographs, physical and digital models, and sketches that help illustrate the design process of the concert hall. Aside from the index there are photos and videos throughout the experience that are used to give more detail about information spoken about/written about in the article sections. The site uses familiar visual language from other mediums at a few points - the article sections are reminicisent of traditional books/magazines, the video sections are similar to any other typical video player and the 3d models are controlled in a way that would be familiar to anyone with experience playing a 3d game or using a 3d model software.

10) What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

I think the main point of the photos and videos in the article are to further inform and engage the user in the story being told. The use of familiar styles of interaction seems mainly in the name of allowing easy access to the information. The user is never unsure of what to do because the site makes it clear what to do in order to continue through its content. The article sections are obviously intended to be scrolled through like any typical news site. The video sections are intended to be watched and can be skipped through or revisited in ways similar to other video players. The 3D model interactions have clear instructions but are also intuitive to those familiar, allowing an experienced user to easily experience the site to its full effect.

11) What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

I think the article sections most affected my feelings about the site - The format and content of those sections felt the most like a professionally written piece documenting the creation of this building. They made engaging with the site feel important and educational to me.

12) What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

In my opinion, the most frustrating part of the interaction was my inability to access the site propetly on platforms other than a desktop web browser. It would have been easier for me to do this assignment if I could have viewed the site on my iPad, the platform I use most often. I understand adding this compatibility would greatly increase the complexity of the site, but it would make it a lot more widely accessible.

13) What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

I personally found scrolling to alter the drone footage the most satisfying part of the interaction. I'm not sure how much of it was intentional, but it's simply fun to watch the sweeping shots reverse and pan backwards and forwards through very little effort from the user.
